When the user provides a complete draft of something (script, plan, outline, email), my job is to **enhance** not **replace**. Keep their structure, voice, specific examples, and tone. Layer framework improvements (hook, rule planting, transitions, payoff beats, data callouts) on top of what they wrote.

**Why:** User gave me a full 20-minute YouTube script on 2026-04-23 with their own structure (6 sections), voice ("5-year-old could understand"), analogies ("magical folders," "robot team," "ghost intern"), and specific examples (anime dashboard, 290-page WEF report, Adobe Premiere demo, 6AM Gmail scheduler). Their explicit ask: *"make and add data into this and use our frameworks to flow better."* Not: "rewrite it in your voice." When users have done writing work, overriding it — even with "better" prose — wastes their work and signals I didn't listen.

**How to apply:**
- When user sends a draft, read it fully before editing. Identify: their structure, their voice tells, their examples, their tone.
- Preserve EVERY specific example they gave — swap none, invent none.
- Framework upgrades go in the margins / transitions / intro / outro / structural wrappers — not in the middle of their prose.
- Common enhancements to layer on: (1) cold-demo hook if missing, (2) decision-rule plant by 55s (Speed-to-List), (3) rule repeat 3× mid + end, (4) Use/Skip pairs per section, (5) payoff recap / decision table at the end, (6) clean transitions between sections, (7) 1-2 light data callouts from research, (8) TNT match check in first 15 sec.
- Never "improve" their voice. If their tone is playful, don't make it operator-calm. If their tone is technical, don't make it accessible. Match.
- Show diff explicitly when delivering: "Here's what I kept (yours) and here's what I added (framework)." User can then see respect for their work.
